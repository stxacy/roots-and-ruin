Cascading Consequences

When forests fall, everything connected to them falls too.

Climate

Forests absorb the carbon we emit. Without them, we're in freefall.

Tropical forests alone store over 250 billion tonnes of carbon. When a tree is burned or left to rot after logging, that carbon re-enters the atmosphere — accelerating the warming that is already reshaping weather patterns, sea levels, and ecosystems worldwide.

Biodiversity

Half of Earth's species live in forests. Their extinction is permanent.

Over 50% of the world's plant and animal species inhabit tropical forests, most yet undiscovered. As canopy cover shrinks, habitats fragment and species that cannot adapt fast enough vanish — taking with them genetic diversity, ecosystem services, and potential medicines we haven't found yet.

Water

Forests are the source of most freshwater. We are cutting off the tap.

Trees regulate the water cycle — drawing moisture from the ground and releasing it back into the atmosphere as rain. Deforestation in headwater regions causes rivers to shrink and rainfall patterns to shift, threatening the agricultural systems and drinking water of billions of people hundreds of kilometres away.

People

Forest communities lose their homes, food, and futures — first.

Indigenous and forest-dependent communities, who have stewarded these ecosystems for millennia, bear the harshest immediate consequences. Land-grabs by agricultural and extractive industries displace millions, erasing cultures and knowledge systems that cannot be recovered. They are the frontline — and they are fighting back.

What Works

The problem is solvable. The tools exist. What's missing is will.

Deforestation is not inevitable. Countries, communities, and companies have already demonstrated that forests can be protected, restored, and governed fairly — at scale.

01
Indigenous Land Rights
Legally recognising Indigenous territories is the single most effective forest protection strategy known.
02
Supply Chain Transparency
Mandating deforestation-free certification for soy, palm oil, beef, timber, and cocoa eliminates demand for cleared land.
03
REDD+ Finance
Paying forest nations for carbon sequestration makes standing forests economically competitive with clearing them.
04
Forest Restoration at Scale
The Bonn Challenge commits 61 nations to restoring 350 million hectares by 2030 — restoring watersheds and corridors.
05
Satellite Monitoring & Enforcement
Near real-time forest monitoring via Global Forest Watch makes illegal clearing visible within days — enabling faster prosecution.
